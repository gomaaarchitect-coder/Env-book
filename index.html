Chapter 1 · Pillar foundations

The Environmental Design Concept

Why environmental design is the core of architecture — and why every line you draw is an environmental decision.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Explain why environmental design is central to architecture.
  • Describe the environment as an active system, not a background.
  • Recognise that every architectural decision has an environmental consequence.
● In Class

Not “green features” — a life-support system

This course is not about bolting “green features” onto a finished design. It is about designing buildings for humans and nature from the first sketch.

The opening thought experiment: you are sealed inside a perfectly insulated building on a damaged planet. In one minute you must list the things you need to control to survive — oxygen, CO₂, temperature, water, food, energy, waste — and rank them. In doing this you have just designed a life-support system for human comfort and health.

1
Environmental architecture does exactly the same thing: it is a designed built-environment that protects human comfort and health.
2
The built environment is a controller of the people–nature relationship. If it blocks excessive heat it is good; if it also blocks the view it is bad. Environmental design is finding that balance.
3
Environmental architecture also cares for the natural environment — in nature, resources regulate themselves; good design works with those cycles instead of fighting them.
● In Class

The four pillars of the course

  1. Life between buildings — how buildings affect natural forces.
  2. Nature protection — how to design for biodiversity.
  3. Comfort & the human–nature relationship — naturally cool buildings with ventilation, daylight and views.
  4. Building physics — calculating these forces to see exactly how your design affects comfort and health.

“Every line you draw is an environmental decision.”

◆ Further Readings

The environment as an active system

Buildings as energy exchangers

A building is never neutral. It constantly exchanges heat, light, air and moisture with its surroundings. Engineers describe this with an energy balance: heat gains (sun, people, equipment) must be matched by heat losses (conduction, ventilation) or the interior drifts out of comfort. Every façade decision shifts this balance — which is why the course later teaches you to quantify it (OTTV, ADF, ACH).

Comfort is a range, not a number

Human thermal comfort depends on air temperature, radiant temperature, humidity, air speed and clothing/activity. The adaptive comfort model shows people in naturally ventilated buildings accept a wider temperature range when they have control (an openable window, a breeze). This is the scientific reason natural ventilation and daylight “feel” good — a theme that returns in Chapters 10–11.

Connect this to Chapter 7 (OTTV) where the energy balance becomes a number you can design against.

✎ Training Exercises

1“Seeing the Invisible.” Choose one campus building and map it: What environmental forces (sun, wind) act on it? How do they change over the day and year? Where are the pleasant and unpleasant spots, and what one design change would improve it?
Model approach: Sketch the building in plan. Draw the summer and winter sun paths (east→south→west) and mark which façades overheat in afternoon. Add the prevailing wind arrow and note where it is blocked or funnelled. “Nice” spots are usually shaded + breezy (north side, under trees); “bad” spots are west-facing glass with no shading. A strong single change names a force and a strategy, e.g. “add a horizontal shade to the west café glazing to cut afternoon heat gain.”
Mark complete
2In your own words, why is “add green features at the end” a weak strategy compared with environmental design from the start? Give one concrete example.
Model approach: Late “green features” treat symptoms (e.g. bolt-on solar panels to offset an over-glazed, overheating west façade) while the underlying form still wastes energy. Designing from the start lets the form itself solve the problem — orient the long axis east–west, shrink west glazing, shade it — so less energy is needed in the first place. The decision is cheaper, more durable and architecturally integrated.
Mark complete
Chapter 2 · Pillar 1

The Design Concept

The single guiding idea that organises every decision — the DNA of your building.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Formulate an environmental concept from site data.
  • Write a concept statement using the course equation.
  • Generate building masses that respond to one or more site forces.
● In Class

What is a design concept?

A design concept is a guiding principle that organises design decisions — the DNA of your building. It is the one answer to all questions: when you are unsure whether to do X or Y, the concept decides.

The course gives you a concrete formula for writing one:

Concept = Design intention  +  Environmental force  +  Spatial strategy

Example: “Due to the cool northern wind, the building takes a V-shape with large openings connecting north and south.” — intention (catch the breeze) + force (north wind) + strategy (V-shape, cross openings).

1
Remove the concept and the building loses its architecture.
2
The site defines everything.
3
An environmental force can be natural (sun, wind, view) or man-made (a noisy road, a neighbour, a special view).

Weak “concepts” such as “my building is wavy / modern” fail the test — they name a shape, not a force-driven response.

● In Class · Key examples

Concepts in built work

Diriyah Contemporary Art Hub and the Kö-Bogen II mixed-use project in Düsseldorf are studied as buildings whose form is legibly driven by an environmental idea rather than arbitrary styling — the massing, openings and surfaces all answer the same concept.

In class you generate 5 building masses for a given site: first responding to one force, then to two, then a full concept statement that follows the equation.

◆ Further Readings

From parti to performance

The “parti” tradition

Architects have long used a parti — a single organising diagram — to keep a project coherent. The course equation is a performance-aware version: it forces the parti to name a real environmental force, so the poetic idea and the physics stay coupled from day one.

Concept as a decision filter

A good concept is testable. For every later choice — glazing ratio, shading depth, opening position — you should be able to ask “does this strengthen the concept and the force it answers?” If not, it is decoration. This is exactly why Chapters 7–11 attach numbers (OTTV, ADF, ACH) to the concept.

✎ Training Exercises

1Rewrite each weak idea as a proper concept statement using the equation: (a) “my building is wavy”, (b) “my building is modern”.
Model approach: (a) “To funnel the lake breeze through the plan (intention) against the prevailing summer wind (force), the floor plates wave in section to open windward and close leeward (strategy).” (b) Replace “modern” with a force: “To exploit the unobstructed southern view and winter sun (force) while rejecting the western afternoon glare (force), the mass steps back to the west and cantilevers a shaded south terrace (strategy).” The test: a reader can name the force you answered.
Mark complete
2For a site with a park to the south, a noisy street to the west and prevailing north wind, sketch 5 distinct masses driven by one chosen force, then 2 masses driven by two forces together.
Model approach: Pick the north wind: masses might (1) form a V to catch it, (2) lift on pilotis to let it pass under, (3) carve a central wind slot, (4) angle louvred north openings, (5) terrace down to the south so the breeze washes over. For two forces (wind + street noise) combine a solid acoustic western spine with open, breeze-catching north faces. High marks = clear response, statement follows the equation, quality of drawings, and discussion.
Mark complete
Chapter 3 · Pillar 2

Biodiversity

Using living systems to do real environmental jobs — and as a generator for the concept.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Distinguish biodiversity design from cosmetic “greening”.
  • Name the environmental jobs living systems can perform.
  • Use biodiversity as a driver of spatial and environmental systems.
● In Class

Biodiversity vs. greening

Biodiversity is the variation of species in an area, and — with habitat — a key factor in environmental balance. Architecture always affects biodiversity, positively or negatively, and it operates at many levels: interior, building, city block, street and whole city.

Greening adds vegetation chosen mainly for a visual or feel-good effect. Biodiversity design starts from a different question: “What environmental jobs need to be done, and can living systems do them?” — and species choice must suit that function.

★
Biodiversity is the idea that design can be a tool to restore nature instead of removing it — and a driver for the concept.
● In Class

Five environmental jobs living systems can do

  1. Thermal protection — e.g. New Street Square, London.
  2. Protection from noise & pollution — e.g. Ian Martin Group offices.
  3. Regulation of microclimate — e.g. Central Park, New York.
  4. Habitat restoration & connectivity — e.g. Hunter’s Point South Waterfront Park.
  5. Urban-heat-island & stormwater management — e.g. Ketcheson Neighbourhood Park.

The Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect: urban materials store heat during the day and release it after sunset, keeping cities hotter than their surroundings. Living systems cool through shade and evapotranspiration.

Key examples studied: Paley Park (NY), the Optical Glass House (Hiroshima), Steno Diabetes Center (Copenhagen), Salesforce Transit Center (San Francisco).

◆ Further Readings

How plants actually cool and clean

Evapotranspiration & shade

Plants cool in two ways: they block solar radiation before it hits a surface, and they convert heat into latent energy as water evaporates from leaves (evapotranspiration). A mature street tree can have a cooling effect equivalent to several air-conditioners running — at no energy cost.

Green roofs, walls & stormwater

Vegetated roofs reduce roof-surface temperatures dramatically, insulate, and absorb stormwater, easing peak loads on drainage. The design lesson is to match the system to the job: a biodiverse, deep-substrate roof for habitat and water retention behaves very differently from a thin sedum mat chosen only for looks.

Ecosystem services as a design brief

The wider framing is ecosystem services — quantifiable benefits (cooling, air cleaning, pollination, flood control) that nature provides. Treating these as design requirements turns “landscaping” into infrastructure.

✎ Training Exercises

1Use biodiversity as the concept for a given site, responding to all its forces. Draw the site plan and three 3D sketches; write the concept statement using the equation.
Model approach: Assign each force a living-system job: a dense planted western buffer for thermal + noise protection, a green roof for UHI/stormwater, and a habitat corridor linking the park through the site. Concept statement, e.g.: “To restore the severed park-to-street habitat corridor (intention) against UHI and western road noise (forces), the building splits into two planted bars bridged by a biodiverse roof (strategy).”
Mark complete
2For each of the five environmental jobs, name one species or planting strategy and justify why it suits the function (not just the look).
Model approach (examples): Thermal — broad deciduous canopy (shade in summer, sun in winter). Noise/pollution — dense evergreen hedge with rough leaves to trap particulates year-round. Microclimate — water + tall shade trees for evaporative cooling. Habitat/connectivity — native flowering species that link to existing corridors and feed local pollinators. UHI/stormwater — deep-substrate green roof + rain garden to store and slowly release water. Each choice is justified by the job.
Mark complete
Chapter 4 · Argument & advocacy

The Biodiversity Debate

How do you argue for biodiversity when the pressure is always to cut it first?

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Argue for or against biodiversity requirements from four stakeholder perspectives.
  • Anticipate and counter opposing arguments.
  • Use an LLM as a debate coach without outsourcing your thinking.
● In Class

The simulation

Parliament puts forward a motion: “Biodiversity requirements must be a non-negotiable, fully funded component of all major urban development projects in Egypt.” You argue from one of four roles:

  • Developers
  • Designers
  • Environmentalists
  • Financial sector

Phase 1 (individual): in-class debate on the online platform. Phase 2 (groups of 5): record a 15–20 minute podcast discussing the topic from the different perspectives.

CriterionWeight
Depth & quality of arguments40%
Engagement with opposing perspectives30%
Presentation quality & creativity30%
● In Class

Using your LLM as a debate coach

The course gives you a structured prompt to prepare, not to replace your thinking. Step through it one section at a time:

  1. A neutral 200-word briefing on the topic.
  2. How your role specifically views the topic.
  3. The likely positions of the other three groups.
  4. Three sharp counterarguments against those positions.
  5. References you can cite.
  6. A 60-second opening statement.

Then a second prompt makes the LLM simulate the three opponents, one at a time, challenging you and giving coaching feedback after each exchange.

This is the same retrieval-and-rehearsal logic the Live Assistant in this book is built around.

◆ Further Readings

The economics behind the argument

Why biodiversity gets “cut first”

Green elements are usually a visible up-front cost while their benefits (cooling, health, flood protection) are diffuse and delayed. In a value-engineering exercise the easy cut is the thing whose value isn't on the balance sheet. The strongest pro-biodiversity arguments therefore monetise the benefits: avoided cooling energy, higher rents and sale values for green-adjacent space, reduced stormwater infrastructure, and health-driven productivity.

Natural capital & policy

Frameworks such as natural capital accounting and biodiversity net-gain policies try to put nature on the books so it survives the value-engineering meeting. Knowing this vocabulary lets the “financial sector” and “developer” roles argue credibly on their own terms.

✎ Training Exercises

1Write a 60-second opening statement for your assigned role, then list the three strongest counterarguments you expect from the others.
Model approach: Lead with your role's core value (developer: cost/return; environmentalist: ecological survival; designer: quality of place; finance: risk and asset value), give one piece of evidence, then a memorable close. For counterarguments, attack the weakest assumption of each opponent — e.g. against the developer, “the cost you cite ignores avoided cooling energy and resale premiums.” Rehearse out loud against the AI opponent simulation.
Mark complete
2Steel-man the opposite side: write the single best argument against your own position, then refute it.
Model approach: A strong opposing point to a pro-mandate stance: “A blanket, fully-funded mandate raises housing costs and slows badly-needed development.” Refutation: target the mandate's design, not the goal — tiered requirements by project size, public co-funding, and counting long-term savings — so you concede the concern while keeping the principle. Engaging opposition seriously is worth 30% of the grade.
Mark complete
Chapter 5 · Pillar 3 & 4

Sun & Solar Geometry

Reading the sun's path so you can anticipate exactly how it strikes a building.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Read the sun's path and how daytime length changes through the year.
  • Locate the sun with two angles: altitude and azimuth.
  • Anticipate how the sun hits each façade at a given date and time.
● In Class

Sun brings light — and heat

The sun gives light but also heat, so design has strategies to manage it: orientation, building geometry, thermal protection and solar control. Because the sun is always moving, a designer must know exactly how it strikes the building to respond correctly.

☀
No two days have the same daytime length. Summer solstice (21 Jun) = longest day; winter solstice (21 Dec) = shortest; equinox (21 Mar & 21 Sep) = day equals night.

Latitude lines (90°, 66.5°, 23.5°, equator, …) explain why the sun's height changes with both season and place.

● In Class

Two angles locate the sun

The complex solar geometry is simplified into two angles for design:

  • Altitude angle — how high the sun is above the horizon (vertical).
  • Azimuth angle — the sun's compass direction (horizontal), measured from north/south.

With these two numbers you can draw the sun's position and trace how its rays meet each surface. The in-class task: draw the sun and how it hits the building's surfaces at 10am, 12pm and 4pm at equinox.

These two angles return directly in Chapter 9 (Solar Control), where you size shading to block both the altitude and azimuth components of a ray.

◆ Further Readings

Sun-path diagrams & why west is worst

Reading a sun-path diagram

A sun-path (stereographic) diagram plots altitude and azimuth for every hour and month at a given latitude on one chart. Architects overlay it on a plan to read shading and solar access quickly — it is the graphical companion to the two angles you learn here.

Why the west façade overheats

At solar noon the sun is high (large altitude), so a modest overhang shades a south window easily. In late afternoon the sun is low in the west (small altitude) and shines almost horizontally under any overhang, straight onto west glass — while the air is already at its daily peak. That combination is why west-facing glazing is the classic overheating problem, and why it needs vertical, not horizontal, shading.

Hot-climate rule

In a hot climate the mass should reject summer radiation while admitting a controlled amount in winter — the seasonal asymmetry the solstice diagrams make visible.

✎ Training Exercises

1Draw the sun's location and how it hits each façade at 10am, 12pm and 4pm at the equinox. Mark which surfaces receive direct sun at each time.
Model approach: At equinox the sun rises due east, is due south at noon, sets due west. 10am — low-ish in the south-east, lighting east + south façades. 12pm — high in the south, lighting south (and roof). 4pm — low in the south-west, lighting south + west. North stays in shade all day at this latitude. Show the altitude rising to noon then falling, and the azimuth sweeping east→south→west.
Mark complete
2Explain, using altitude and azimuth, why a horizontal overhang protects a south window in summer but fails on a west window in the afternoon.
Model approach: Summer-noon south sun has high altitude, so its rays come from steeply above — a horizontal overhang easily intercepts them. Afternoon west sun has low altitude and a westerly azimuth, so rays travel nearly horizontally beneath the overhang and strike the glass. West windows therefore need vertical fins or screens that block the low, side-on azimuth, not a horizontal shelf.
Mark complete
Chapter 6 · Pillar 3

Orientation

Turning the building, its zones and its spaces to work with the sun and wind.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Distinguish mass, zonal and spatial orientation.
  • Orient a hot-climate mass to reject summer sun and admit winter sun.
  • Resolve conflicting factors (ventilation, daylight, shading, view) at the space scale.
● In Class

Three scales of orientation

  • Building mass orientation — how the whole volume sits on the site. In a hot climate, design the mass to reject as much summer radiation as possible while allowing a controlled amount in winter. The same floor area at different orientations (e.g. 6 m × 30 m vs 15 m × … facing different ways) performs very differently.
  • Zonal orientation — placing functions with particular needs in the parts of the building that can supply those conditions (e.g. heat-tolerant services to the west, sensitive offices to the north).
  • Spatial orientation — at the single-space scale, finding a solution to conflicting factors: natural ventilation, daylighting, shading, view and energy all at once.
↻
Orientation is a free move: rotating and zoning the same building can cut heat gain and improve daylight and views at no extra material cost.

Studied examples: ZGF Architects' office in Agoura Hills; PDEC (passive downdraught evaporative cooling) ventilation; and One Hyde Park as an orientation analysis.

◆ Further Readings

The east–west long axis & daylight depth

Why elongate east–west

A building stretched on an east–west axis presents its long faces to north and south. South sun is high and easy to shade with horizontal devices; north light is soft and glare-free. The short east and west faces — the hardest to shade because of low sun — are minimised. This single move underlies most passive hot-climate plans.

Zoning as a thermal buffer

Zonal orientation can use less-sensitive rooms (stairs, stores, services) as a thermal buffer on the hot west side, shielding occupied spaces — a free layer of protection before any insulation is added.

Daylight reach

Useful daylight penetrates only about 2–2.5× the window head height into a room (Chapter 10). Narrow, well-oriented floor plates therefore daylight naturally; deep plans force artificial light and bigger cooling loads.

✎ Training Exercises

1Given the same floor area, compare two mass orientations for a hot climate. Which rejects more summer heat while keeping winter sun, and why?
Model approach: The orientation with its long axis east–west wins: large south and north faces (easy to shade / soft light), small east and west faces (hard, low-sun exposure minimised). It admits controllable high winter south sun while a horizontal shade blocks high summer south sun. The north–south-elongated option exposes big east/west walls to harsh low morning and afternoon sun — more summer gain.
Mark complete
2Design a concept for a 10,000 m² office (70% offices, 30% services) using only zonal orientation, with biodiversity and sun angles as generators. State which zone goes where and why.
Model approach: Put offices on the north and shaded south for stable daylight and views to the lake; bank services, cores and circulation along the hot west as a thermal buffer. Use the summer June sun path (10/12/2/4) to mark west “danger zones” and a planted screen there; use the December path to keep south winter sun reaching offices. Concept statement ties plants + sun angles to the massing.
Mark complete
Chapter 7 · Pillar 4 · Building physics

Building Skin & OTTV

Controlling the thermal performance of the envelope — and proving it with a number.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Explain the skin energy balance.
  • Compute OTTV surface-by-surface and per floor area.
  • Test interventions to bring OTTV/floor area below the target.
● In Class

Skin energy balance

Energy balance is the strategic optimisation of a building's energy use and environmental impact against the comfort, health and well-being of its occupants. The skin is where most of that exchange happens, so we quantify it with the Overall Thermal Transfer Value (OTTV).

For each surface, heat transfer combines conduction through the opaque wall and glazing plus solar gain through the glazing:

Qsurface = (Aw·Uwall·ΔT) + (Ag·Uglass·ΔT) + (Ag·SHGC·SC·Isol)

Sum every surface, divide by floor area, and compare against the target. The in-class case: north/south walls 42 m² (WWR 0.3), east/west 28 m², roof 96 m²; wall U = 1.68, glazing U = 5.0, roof U = 0.54.

⚑
Target: OTTV / floor area < 100 W/m². Above it, the design fails.
● In Class · Predict → Calculate → Validate

The 5-part workflow

  1. Predict: without calculating, rank the surfaces from highest to lowest heat gain, with reasoning.
  2. Calculate: hand-compute OTTV surface by surface and test your hypothesis.
  3. Hypothesise: propose just two interventions to drop OTTV/floor area the most.
  4. Validate: use the calculator below to test it — if it stays above 100 W/m², mark it Fail (in red).
  5. Reflect: one short paragraph on where your hypothesis was right or wrong.

◆ Further Readings

U-value, WWR and where the heat really goes

The U-value, demystified

A U-value (W/m²K) is the rate heat passes through 1 m² of a construction per 1 °C difference — lower is better. It is the reciprocal of total thermal resistance (U = 1/ΣR), so adding insulation layers (high R) drives U down. Note the in-class glazing U of 5.0 versus wall 1.68: glass leaks roughly three times faster than the wall, which is why window choices dominate the result.

Window-to-Wall Ratio (WWR)

OTTV is extremely sensitive to WWR and to which façade the glazing sits on. Solar gain through glazing — not conduction — is usually the largest term on sunny façades, so the highest-leverage interventions are reducing west/east WWR and improving the glazing's solar control (low-SHGC glass or external shading), more than thickening opaque walls.

Why per-floor-area?

Dividing by floor area normalises buildings of different sizes so the <100 W/m² target is a fair benchmark — it rewards compact, well-shaded envelopes, linking straight to Chapter 8's SA/V idea.

✎ Training Exercises

1Before any maths, rank the case-study surfaces (N, S, E, W, roof) from highest to lowest heat gain and justify. Then hand-calculate OTTV per surface and check your ranking.
Model approach: Predict the roof and the sun-exposed glazed façades (south, then west/east) as worst because of high solar gain and the leaky U=5 glass; north is lowest (no direct sun). Compute each Q with the formula above, sum, divide by floor area. Compare to your ranking — surprises usually come from WWR differences or the roof's large area. Honest reflection on the mismatch is the point.
Mark complete
2Propose exactly two interventions to push OTTV/floor area below 100 W/m². Validate them in the calculator above. If it still fails, say why and revise.
Model approach: The two highest-leverage moves are almost always (1) cut WWR / add external shading on the worst solar façade, and (2) swap to low-SHGC, lower-U glazing. Thickening opaque walls helps little because the glass dominates. Run both in the tool; if still >100, the solar-gain term is still too high — deepen the shading or reduce glazing further rather than touching the walls.
Mark complete
Chapter 8 · Pillar 4

Building Geometry (SA/V)

The shape of your plan changes how much skin trades heat with the outside.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Show that equal volumes can have very different surface areas.
  • Compute the surface-area-to-volume (SA/V) ratio.
  • Trade off compactness against daylight, view and ventilation.
● In Class

Same volume, different skin

Studying geometry lets you choose intelligently among design alternatives. The key insight: the same cubic metres of space can be wrapped in different surface areas. The building skin offers daylight, views and ventilation — but it also exchanges heat with the outside far more easily than the core.

So the shape of your plan changes the external skin area, and therefore the heat that can pass through it. Compare a 100 m² floor as a square (10×10), a thin rectangle, an L, or a circle — each has a different perimeter and skin.

SA/V ratio = total external surface area  /  enclosed volume   (lower = more compact = less heat exchange)
◧
Compact forms (low SA/V) lose/gain less heat; articulated forms (high SA/V) get more daylight, view and cross-ventilation. Design is the trade-off.
◆ Further Readings

The compactness trade-off, in context

Climate decides the direction

In hot or cold climates with big indoor–outdoor temperature gaps, a compact (low SA/V) form saves energy — less skin to leak through. In mild climates, a more articulated (high SA/V) form can win by harvesting daylight and cross-ventilation, cutting lighting and cooling loads. The “best” shape is climate-dependent.

SA/V meets OTTV

SA/V and Chapter 7's OTTV are two views of the same physics: SA/V sets how much skin you have, OTTV sets how good each square metre is. A compact form with a modest envelope can beat a sprawling form with an expensive one — and costs less.

The thin-plan exception

A deliberately thin plan raises SA/V but is often justified: every space gets a window for daylight and cross-ventilation, slashing lighting and mechanical-cooling energy. The skill is knowing when the daylight/ventilation gain outweighs the extra envelope loss.

✎ Training Exercises

1Design three variations of one building mass: keep the volume constant but increase SA/V once and decrease it once. Show the surface-area and volume calculation for each.
Model approach: Hold volume fixed (e.g. 1000 m³). A cube (10×10×10) has SA = 600 m² → SA/V = 0.6 (compact baseline). A thin slab (5×40×5) raises SA to ~1300 m² → SA/V ≈ 1.3 (more skin, more daylight). A taller, near-cubic block lowers SA toward the cube minimum. Tabulate SA, V and SA/V for all three and state the comfort/energy implication of each.
Mark complete
2For a hot climate, argue whether you'd pick the most compact form or a slightly less compact one — and how Chapter 10 (daylight) and 11 (ventilation) change your answer.
Model approach: Pure conduction logic favours the most compact form to reject heat. But a slightly thinner plan lets daylight reach ~2–2.5× the window head height (Ch.10) and enables cross-ventilation for night cooling (Ch.11), cutting lighting and cooling energy that often outweighs the extra skin. The defensible answer balances a moderately compact mass with daylight/ventilation access, not the extreme of either.
Mark complete
Chapter 9 · Pillar 3 & 4

Solar Control

Shading — keeping the sun's rays off the glazing using altitude and azimuth.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Decide when and where a façade needs shading.
  • Choose a device type for each orientation.
  • Size a device from the solar altitude and azimuth.
● In Class

Solar control = shading

Architectural solar control is an external device over a window to block the sun — the goal is that solar rays don't fall on the glazing (or large parts of it). The glazing heat balance has two parts:

Qconduction = A·U·ΔT   |   Qsolar gain = A·SHGF·Isol

Two factors drive the design: microclimate and orientation — solar angles differ per façade, so each façade needs a different device. Because angles also change with season, you must first decide whether you're shading in summer, winter, or both.

Device types: screen · horizontal shading · overhang · vertical louvers.

Studied examples: Kimbell Art Museum Expansion (Renzo Piano), UQ Global Change Institute (Brisbane), Missouri Dept. of Natural Resources.

● In Class

How to design & size shading

  1. Identify the glazing areas that need shading.
  2. Identify the seasons and times they should be shaded.
  3. Use solar angles to place the device to shade the whole window (or part).
  4. Calculate the device so it stops rays reaching the glazing — blocking both the azimuth and altitude components.

The geometry: a horizontal device depth is set by the altitude; a vertical fin by the azimuth. For a low, side-on sun you need fins; for a high sun, an overhang.

◆ Further Readings

Matching the device to the sun

The horizontal-shadow-angle / vertical-shadow-angle pair

Designers formalise shading with two angles: the vertical shadow angle (how the altitude projects onto a section, set by overhang depth) and the horizontal shadow angle (how the azimuth projects on plan, set by fin spacing). A device is “designed” when its geometry produces shadow angles that cover the required sun positions.

Right device, right façade

South (high sun): horizontal overhangs/louvres work best. East & west (low sun): vertical fins or movable screens, since horizontal shelves can't block near-horizontal rays. North (in shade most of the year at this latitude): usually minimal shading needed. Egg-crate (combined) devices handle façades that face the corner directions.

Fixed vs. movable, and depth vs. daylight

Deeper devices shade more but also cut daylight and view — the link to Chapter 10. Movable louvres, perforated screens and seasonal geometry (blocking high summer sun while admitting low winter sun) resolve this tension.

✎ Training Exercises

1Design the device to fully shade a 2×2 m east window when (a) solar altitude 70°, azimuth 90°, and (b) altitude 50°, azimuth 100°. Give the geometry for each.
Model approach: East sun is low and side-on, so lead with vertical fins set by the azimuth; add a top horizontal element for the altitude. Depth from altitude: a horizontal projection P shades a window of height H when P = H / tan(altitude). For 70°: P = 2/tan70 ≈ 0.73 m. For 50°: P = 2/tan50 ≈ 1.68 m — much deeper because the sun is lower. The fin spacing/projection follows from the azimuth offset from the façade normal. Verify in the tool.
Mark complete
2Why does the device get deeper as the solar altitude drops, and what does that imply for east/west versus south shading?
Model approach: Required projection P = H/tan(altitude); as altitude falls, tan(altitude) falls, so P grows — a low sun demands a deep (often impractical) horizontal device. South sun is high, so shallow overhangs suffice. East/west sun is low, so horizontal devices become absurdly deep and block daylight; therefore use vertical fins or movable screens there instead. This is the quantitative reason for the device-per-orientation rule.
Mark complete
Chapter 10 · Pillar 3 & 4

Daylighting & ADF

Bringing in useful, diffuse light — without the focused heat of direct sun.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Distinguish daylight (skylight) from sunlight.
  • Calculate the Average Daylight Factor (ADF).
  • Apply daylight design criteria and run a sensitivity analysis.
● In Class

Daylight vs. sunlight

Skylight is diffuse and even, present on overcast and clear days — good for work. Sunlight is a focused beam of light and heat, limited to clear days and orientation-dependent, creating bright and dark patches and warmth on cold days but glare on work surfaces.

Daylight is measured by the Daylight Factor (DF) — the percentage of external light reaching a specific interior point — averaged across the space as the Average Daylight Factor (ADF).

%
A healthy ADF is between 3% and 6%.
ADF = (W · θ · T)  /  [ Atotal · (1 − R²) ]  × C

where W = window area, θ = angle of visible sky, T = glazing transmittance, Atotal = area of all internal surfaces, R = average reflectance.

● In Class

Angle of visible sky & design criteria

The angle of visible sky is measured from the window centre, between the obstruction opposite the window and the one above it — the slice of sky the window can actually “see”. Balconies sharply reduce it, especially in dense surroundings; painting external obstructions a light colour is worth roughly +6° of visible sky.

Design criteria: room depth ≈ 2–2.5× the window head height; distribute windows across the wall for even light. Light can also come from above via skylights (horizontal glazing), bulkhead lighting, and roof monitors.

Studied example: Wayne L. Morse U.S. Courthouse.

◆ Further Readings

Beyond the daylight factor

Why the surface reflectance term matters so much

The (1 − R²) denominator means interior reflectance is a powerful lever: light-coloured walls, ceilings and floors bounce daylight deeper into the room, raising ADF without any extra glass (and therefore without extra solar heat gain). This is the cheapest daylight upgrade available.

The daylight–heat tension

Bigger windows raise ADF but also raise OTTV (Chapter 7) and demand shading (Chapter 9). The art is hitting 3–6% ADF with the least glass — using high transmittance, high reflectance and top-lighting — so daylight and thermal goals are met together. This is exactly the Chapter-10 “Part 3” brief: ADF = 5.5 and east-wall OTTV/area ≤ 60.

Modern metrics

Practice now also uses climate-based daylight metrics (Daylight Autonomy, Useful Daylight Illuminance) that account for the real annual sky and glare — but ADF remains the quick, hand-calculable design check you learn here.

✎ Training Exercises

1A 10×10×3.6 m room has a 2 m² window (T = 0.55), average R = 0.5 and θ = 25°. Find the ADF and judge the light coverage. Is it healthy?
Model approach: Compute total internal surface area (2 walls+floor+ceiling minus window), plug W, θ, T, R into the formula and solve for ADF (use the calculator to confirm). With only 2 m² of glass in a large room, expect an ADF well below the healthy 3–6% band — so it's under-lit. Coverage: useful daylight reaches only ≈2–2.5× the window head height from the wall, leaving the room's far side dark.
Mark complete
2Sensitivity analysis: increase each variable in turn — W (3→4 m²), T (0.65→0.85), θ (45→65°), R (0.65→0.8). Which moves ADF most, and why?
Model approach: ADF rises ~linearly with W, θ and T (double the window ≈ double ADF). The reflectance R often has outsized impact because it sits in the non-linear (1−R²) denominator — raising R from 0.65 to 0.8 sharply increases ADF without adding any solar heat gain. Run each change one-at-a-time in the calculator and rank the deltas; R and W usually lead.

3Design a daylighting scheme meeting two targets at once: ADF = 5.5 and east-wall OTTV/area = 60 W/m². How do you satisfy both?
Model approach: Hit ADF with quality not quantity of glass: high-transmittance, low-SHGC glazing + very light interior surfaces (high R) + top-lighting (skylight/roof monitor) so you need less east glass. Keeping east WWR modest and adding external shading holds east OTTV/area at 60. Iterate both numbers in the Ch.7 and Ch.10 calculators until both pass — the explicit daylight↔heat trade-off.

Chapter 11 · Pillar 3 & 4

Natural Ventilation

Using the laws of physics to drive fresh air through a building — for free.

By the end of this chapter you can…

  • Explain the mechanisms of natural ventilation.
  • Quantify airflow, air change rate and the stack effect.
  • Design and redesign a room for healthy ventilation and summer cooling.
● In Class

What it is and why it matters

Ventilation delivers fresh air to occupants — naturally, mechanically or hybrid. Natural ventilation uses the laws of physics to drive airflow between outside and inside. It cuts energy use, connects occupants to the outdoors (a psychological benefit), removes hot stale air, and improves Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) and health. It also gives a sense of comfort even when temperatures are slightly high.

Two driving forces:

  • Pressure-induced (wind-driven) — single-sided and cross ventilation. Pressure differences around the building push air through.
  • Temperature-induced (buoyancy-driven) — the stack / solar chimney effect; warm air rises and escapes high, drawing cool air in low.
● In Class

Quantifying ventilation

Key quantities: age of air (older = warmer, more polluted), ventilation rate (L/s/person or ACH) and air change rate (ACH = volumes replaced per hour).

Airflow rate   Q = v · A   |   ACH = (Q × 3600) / V
Cross (wind): Q = Cd·A·√(2|ΔP|/ρ)
Stack: Q = Cd·A·√( 2·g·h·(Ti−Te)/(Ti+Te) )

Worked example from class: 4 m/s wind through a 6 m² opening → Q = 24 m³/s; in a 100 m³ room that's a very high ACH. The stack example (Ti=30°C, Te=20°C, A=2 m², h=6 m) shows buoyancy alone can ventilate when there is no wind.

Studied examples: KfW Frankfurt (among the most energy-efficient office towers) and Menara UMNO, Malaysia.

◆ Further Readings

Designing the airflow path

Cross-ventilation depends on the path, not just the openings

Cross ventilation works only to a depth of about 5× the floor-to-ceiling height; beyond that, single-sided ventilation (reaching ~2× the height) takes over. Inlet and outlet should be on opposite pressure zones (windward vs leeward) and roughly equal in area; an undersized outlet throttles the whole flow. Wind hitting a façade obliquely or perpendicular-but-reversed changes the pressure sign and can stall or reverse the path.

The stack effect as a wind-independent backup

When wind fails, buoyancy still works: the bigger the height (h) between inlet and outlet and the bigger the temperature difference, the stronger the draw. A solar chimney deliberately heats its top to amplify ΔT, giving reliable ventilation on still, hot days — the strategy behind Menara UMNO's wind-catchers and KfW's ventilated façade.

Comfort ventilation vs. night cooling

Two distinct jobs: daytime comfort ventilation (air movement over skin makes a warm room feel cooler) and night-flush cooling (cool night air purges heat stored in the mass, pre-cooling the next day). Designing for both, plus IAQ, is the goal of the final exercise.

✎ Training Exercises

1A 10×8 m room (H = 3 m) has a 3×1.2 m sliding window, wind 1.2 m/s. Predict whether it gets healthy natural ventilation, name the type, and say if it's effective — in 5 lines.
Model approach: One openable wall ⇒ single-sided ventilation, which reaches only ≈2×H = 6 m — but the room is 10 m deep and 8 m wide, so the far side is poorly served. Effective area = 3×1.2 = 3.6 m²; at 1.2 m/s the airflow is modest. Verdict: marginal IAQ near the window, weak at the back; for effective ventilation you'd need a second opening on the opposite/leeward wall to drive cross flow.
Mark complete
2Calculate ACH for that room by cross ventilation when outside P = 0.2 Pa and inside P = −0.1 Pa (so ΔP = 0.3 Pa). Use Cd ≈ 0.6, ρ ≈ 1.2 kg/m³. Is the IAQ/cooling healthy?
Model approach: Q = Cd·A·√(2ΔP/ρ) = 0.6 × 3.6 × √(2×0.3/1.2) = 0.6 × 3.6 × √0.5 ≈ 0.6 × 3.6 × 0.707 ≈ 1.53 m³/s. Room V = 10×8×3 = 240 m³. ACH = Q×3600/V = 1.53×3600/240 ≈ 23 ACH — far above the few ACH needed for IAQ, so fresh-air health is easily met; whether it cools depends on the air being cooler than the room and the path actually crossing the occupied zone.
Mark complete
3Redesign the room for summer cooling without changing the pressures; then solve the case where there is no wind at all. Sketch the plan changes and give the ACH logic.
Model approach: With pressure fixed, improve the path: add a leeward outlet opposite the inlet, align openings across the occupied zone, and size the outlet ≥ inlet so flow isn't throttled — this raises effective cross flow and sweeps the whole room. No wind: switch to buoyancy — add a tall stack / solar chimney with a high outlet and low inlet; ACH from the stack equation grows with height h and ΔT, so heat the chimney top and maximise the inlet-to-outlet height. Show both on the plan/section.
Mark complete
